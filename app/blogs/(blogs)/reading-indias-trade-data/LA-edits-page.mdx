_TL;DR: I scraped 7 years of India's bilateral trade data from the Ministry of Commerce. The stories in it are wild — Russia went from supplying 2% of India's energy imports to being #1, China's manufacturing grip tightened despite every political effort to loosen it, and India's phone exports grew tenfold in seven years. Here's what the data actually says._

## The Hook

In 2019, Russia supplied less than 2% of India's mineral fuel imports. By 2025, it was the country's single largest energy supplier.

That's the kind of story buried in trade data — not the headlines about diplomatic summits or sanctions, but the hard numbers showing what actually moved across borders. I scraped seven years of bilateral trade data from India's Ministry of Commerce, covering FY 2018-19 through FY 2024-25, and these are the stories that jumped out.



## Why This Exists

In 2017, I was an undergrad studying economics who wanted to learn Python. I found India's trade data portal, wrote a scraper, uploaded the dataset to Kaggle, and built an R Shiny dashboard. The point was learning to code, not trade analysis. I barely looked at what the data said.

Nine years later, I rebuilt the whole pipeline. Playwright for browser automation, async Python for parallelism, Parquet for storage. What took weeks took hours. But this time I actually _looked_ at the data. And the stories in it are wild.

The shift wasn't just in tooling. I went from "I can scrape a website" to "I can ask questions of the data." AI-assisted development made the engineering trivial — the hard part was knowing which questions to ask. That hasn't been automated yet.

Here's what I found.



## The Macro Picture

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart1.json" />

India's total trade grew about 37% over the seven-year window — exports up 33%, imports up 40%. But the shape of that growth matters more than the number. Imports consistently outpace exports, creating a persistent, structural trade deficit that widened sharply after FY21.

Two things jump out immediately. First, the COVID trough in FY21 — trade contracted on both sides, but snapped back violently as commodity prices surged in FY22. Second, the deficit isn't shrinking. It's getting wider.

One detail worth noting early: India's single largest export is refined petroleum — despite being a massive net oil importer. India imports crude, refines it, and re-exports the products. This creates the illusion of a petroleum powerhouse in export rankings, but the net picture is deeply negative. Keep that in mind as we dig into the deficit.

So where does the deficit actually come from?

## Where the Deficit Lives

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart2.json" />

The diverging bar chart tells the story more directly than any decomposition could. India's bilateral trade balance, country by country, for FY25.

On the right, in green: the US (+$41B), Netherlands (+$18B), Bangladesh (+$10B). Countries where India exports more than it imports — finished goods, refined petroleum, pharmaceuticals, electronics.

On the left, in red: China (-$99B), Russia (-$59B), UAE (-$27B), Iraq (-$26B), Saudi Arabia (-$18B). The structural dependencies: energy, manufacturing inputs, and gold.

The pattern is stark. India earns from value-added exports to rich countries and spends on raw materials and intermediate goods from resource-rich ones. The deficit isn't diffuse — it's concentrated in a handful of relationships. China alone accounts for over a third of the total deficit. Add the oil exporters and you've explained nearly the whole thing.

This sets up the two big stories in the data: Russia (energy dependency) and China (manufacturing dependency).

## The Russia Pivot

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart3.json" />

Before February 2022, India's mineral fuel imports told a stable story. Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE were the big three. Russia was a rounding error — about 2% of India's mineral fuel imports (HS27, which covers crude oil, refined petroleum, coal, and natural gas).

Then Russia invaded Ukraine. Western nations imposed sanctions. The EU embargoed Russian crude. The G7 introduced a price cap. And India saw an opportunity. (For Russia specifically, about 88% of HS27 is crude oil, with the rest being coal and refined products.)

Within two years, Russia went from marginal to dominant. Russia's share of India's mineral fuel imports jumped from under 3% in FY22 to 15% in FY23 and 26% in FY25 — making it the single largest source. The data shows this shift with startling clarity — Russia's band in the stacked area chart goes from barely visible to dominating.

This wasn't ideological alignment. It was pure economic pragmatism. Sanctioned Russian crude traded at steep discounts to Brent benchmarks. India's refineries — already configured to process a range of crude grades — could absorb the supply. The savings flowed through to domestic fuel prices and refinery margins.

The speed of the pivot is what makes the chart striking. Russia's mineral fuel exports to India went from $5B in FY22 to $57B in FY25. Geopolitics reshuffled India's energy map in real time, and the data captures exactly when and how fast it happened.

## The China Paradox

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart4.json" height={750} />

Now for the uncomfortable one.

Despite "Atmanirbhar Bharat" (self-reliance), despite banning Chinese apps after the 2020 Galwan border clash, despite "China+1" becoming a corporate boardroom mantra — India's import dependence on China has _grown_, not shrunk.

The bilateral deficit hit a record ~$99B in FY24-25. The core categories tell the story: electronics (HS85), machinery (HS84), and organic chemicals (HS29 — which are pharmaceutical intermediates). India's electronics manufacturing boom, its capital investment cycle, and its generic pharma industry all depend on Chinese inputs.

The data flatly contradicts the political narrative. Every year since 2020, the rhetoric said "decouple." Every year since 2020, the trade numbers said "more dependent."

This is what makes the next section — India's production-linked incentive scheme — more impressive, not less. India is _trying_ to break free. And in one specific category, it's actually working.

## The PLI Miracle

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart5.json" />

The stacked area chart shows India's export composition over time, and one band is growing faster than everything else: electronics (HS85, in saffron at the bottom).

The Production Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme, launched in 2020, pays companies to manufacture electronics domestically. The results show up clearly — HS85's share of total exports grew from about 6% in FY19 to over 10% in FY25. In absolute terms, electronics exports more than tripled: from $13B to $44B. Phone exports (HS8517) specifically went from $2.6B to $25.9B — a tenfold increase in seven years. Apple now assembles roughly 25% of its iPhones in India.

India is still a net electronics _importer_ — HS85 imports hit $89B in FY25, dwarfing the $44B in exports. But the direction of travel is clear. A country that imported virtually all its phones a decade ago now exports $26B worth of them. That's industrial policy showing up in the trade data.

The nuance: while China dependence grew in _inputs_ (components, chemicals, machinery), India built export capacity in _finished electronics_. The PLI story isn't about decoupling from China. It's about moving up the value chain — importing components and exporting finished products. Structural dependence on Chinese inputs coexists with genuine industrial upgrading in outputs.

## Is India Diversifying?

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart6.json" />

This is the most analytical question in the dataset, and the one where my economics background actually matters.

The chart shows the "effective number" of products and partners — a measure derived from the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI). The intuition: if India exported equally to N countries, the effective number would be N. A higher effective number means more diversification. Think of it as answering "how many equal-sized baskets are India's eggs spread across?"

Two dimensions matter:

**Product diversification.** India's effective number of export products has hovered around 17-18 HS2 chapters throughout the period — meaning India's export concentration is _as if_ it exported equally across about 17 broad product categories. This barely changed despite the PLI-driven electronics surge, because electronics simply replaced gems at the top of the basket rather than broadening it.

**Partner diversification.** This is where the data gets uncomfortable. The effective number of export partners _fell_ from 21 in FY19 to 17 in FY25. India's exports got _more_ concentrated, not less — and almost all of that is the US. America's share of India's exports grew from 16% to 20%, and because HHI squares the shares, that single shift dominates the entire index.

The tension: India is reshuffling _what_ it sells (electronics replacing gems), but concentrating _who_ it sells to. The US is becoming a single point of reliance. Both trends are visible in the data, and they pull in opposite directions.



## India's Trade Atlas

<PlotlyChart src="/blogs/reading-indias-trade-data/chart7.json" />

The map tells the whole story at a glance.

**Green (surplus):** The US, UK, Netherlands, Bangladesh, and much of Africa. India exports more than it imports — finished goods, refined petroleum, pharmaceuticals, IT-adjacent hardware.

**Red (deficit):** China (massive), Russia (post-pivot), the Middle East (energy), and Switzerland (gold — India is the world's largest consumer). These are the structural dependencies: fuel, manufacturing inputs, and precious metals.

**The pattern:** Surpluses with the developed West and South Asia. Deficits with commodity suppliers and China. India earns from value-added exports to rich countries and spends on raw materials and intermediate goods from resource-rich ones.

Every story in this post — the Russia pivot, the China paradox, the PLI miracle, the diversification question — is visible on this single map.



## What Surprised Me

I didn't write this post to summarize India's trade. I wrote it because the data surprised me, and I wanted to understand why.

The Russia pivot was the most visually dramatic — a chart that looks like a regime change, because it is one. The China paradox was the most intellectually uncomfortable — political narratives and economic data telling opposite stories. The PLI numbers were the most optimistic — industrial policy actually working, visible in the export data within three years.

There are things the data can't tell you. Services trade is invisible in goods data — India's massive IT exports don't show up here. Informal trade across land borders is undercounted. Transfer pricing distorts bilateral numbers. The Netherlands shows up as a major trade partner partly because of Rotterdam's role as a re-export hub, not because Dutch consumers are buying Indian goods.

The hard part was the same as in 2017 — knowing what questions to ask. The scraping is faster, the charts are prettier, the tooling is better. But the analytical judgment that says "this number is interesting, that one isn't" — that hasn't been automated yet.
